SO(3) vs SU(2)

Rotational symmetries in physical space form the group SO(3), but quantum states transform under SU(2), its double cover. The correspondence between these groups ensures a \(2:1\) mapping, capturing the fact that a \(360^\circ\) rotation can introduce a sign change in spinor wavefunctions.

Representations and Spin

Irreducible representations of SU(2) are labeled by half-integers \(j\). Each \(j\) yields a \((2j+1)\)-dimensional space of spin states. The familiar spin-\(\frac{1}{2}\) particles reside in the fundamental representation, while spin-1 fields correspond to the adjoint representation.

Ladder Operators

Using the commutation relations \([J_i, J_j] = i\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k\), one constructs ladder operators \(J_{\pm}\) to traverse the eigenbasis of \(J_z\). These operators manifest the algebraic structure that group theory predicts.

Physical Implications

Spin-statistics, selection rules, and coupling schemes all follow from these representation-theoretic foundations. Understanding how group theory constrains spin prepares us to analyze addition of angular momentum and phenomena such as Stern–Gerlach splitting.

Conclusion

Group theory does more than provide abstract structure—it directly informs the quantized nature of spin. Mastery of SU(2) representations gives a powerful language for reasoning about quantum systems and their symmetries.
